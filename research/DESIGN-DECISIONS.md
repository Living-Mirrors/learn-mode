# Design Decisions: Evidence Base for Every Specification Choice

*Every design choice in the Learn Mode specification is traceable to specific research evidence. This document provides the full audit trail.*

---

## How to Read This Document

Each decision is documented with:
- **The decision** as it appears in the specification
- **The research evidence** that supports it
- **Alternative approaches** that were considered
- **The rationale** for the chosen approach

Decisions are grouped by specification section. Within each group, they are ordered by their position in the spec.

## Foundational Architecture Decisions

| # | Decision | Evidence | Alternatives Considered | Rationale |
|---|----------|----------|------------------------|-----------|
| 1 | **Seven principles ordered as the learning sequence** | The ordering follows the actual cognitive sequence of learning: content analysis (what is being learned) before learner adaptation (how to deliver it) before encoding strategies (how to make it stick) before retention mechanisms (how to keep it). Craik & Lockhart (1972): encoding must precede retrieval. Ebbinghaus (1885): the forgetting curve begins at encoding -- retention strategies must follow. | Unordered set; hierarchical tree; parallel activation | Sequential ordering mirrors the empirical learning process. Content must be analyzed before it can be adapted to the learner. Encoding must occur before retrieval can be woven in. The order is not rigid -- all principles are available -- but the default sequence reflects the temporal logic of learning. |
| 2 | **Exactly seven principles (not fewer, not more)** | Each principle maps to a distinct, empirically documented cognitive mechanism with its own research tradition. No two principles describe the same mechanism. No documented mechanism for learning is omitted. | Five (merging Detect with Calibrate, merging Dual Channel with Emotional Encoding); ten (splitting Push Up into separate principles for each encoding level); three (detect, encode, retain) | Seven is the minimum that covers all documented learning mechanisms without redundancy. Merging Detect with Calibrate conflates input (how to assess) with adjustment (how to respond). Splitting encoding levels into separate principles would create seven principles from one mechanism. |
| 3 | **Independent learning axis (Axis 5)** | Learning research describes encoding and retention as distinct from perception (how information is seen), dynamics (how people interact), construction (how things are built), and generation (how novelty arises). These are orthogonal cognitive dimensions. | Single "smart mode" combining learning with other axes; learning as a sub-mode of creative; learning as a setting rather than an axis | The research is clear: encoding mechanisms (Paivio, Craik) are distinct from perceptual processing (lens axis). Memory consolidation (McGaugh) is distinct from creative generation (Koestler). A financial analysis benefits from learning optimization just as much as a creative exploration. Making learning a property of another axis would restrict it artificially. |
| 4 | **Continuous dial (0-100) rather than binary on/off** | Learning intensity is continuous in practice. A person writing a quick email needs less encoding support than someone studying a new domain. Bjork & Bjork (2011): the optimal difficulty level varies by task complexity and learner state. | Binary on/off; three named levels (Light/Medium/Full); fixed at 100% | A continuous dial allows micro-adjustment. Binary activation forces the full suite on tasks that need only light adaptation. Named levels create artificial thresholds. The dial's staggered activation (channel adaptation first, then emotional framing, then generation prompts, then full retrieval) provides internal structure. |

## Principle-Level Decisions

| # | Decision | Evidence | Alternatives Considered | Rationale |
|---|----------|----------|------------------------|-----------|
| 5 | **Detect from behavior instead of self-report questionnaire** | Dunning & Kruger (1999): people systematically misjudge their own competence and cognitive processes. Bjork (1994): current performance is a poor index of learning -- subjective experience of fluency is systematically misleading. The learning styles hypothesis (matching instruction to self-reported preferences) has been tested and found unsupported (Pashler et al., 2008). | Self-report questionnaire ("Are you a visual learner?"); standardized learning assessment; no profiling at all | Self-report produces answers that correlate with preferences, not with actual cognitive architecture. A visual learner questionnaire tells you what someone thinks they prefer, not how their memory system actually encodes. Behavioral observation -- watching how someone naturally teaches -- reveals actual encoding strategy because teaching externalizes the internal representation. |
| 6 | **The profiler uses "teach me something" not a quiz** | Teaching externalizes the learner's mental model. How someone explains reveals their primary channel (narrative, spatial, logical), their emotional engagement pattern, and their tolerance for complexity. A quiz measures what someone knows, not how they encode. The "teach me something you know deeply" prompt ensures the person operates from expertise, where their natural encoding style is most visible. | Knowledge quiz; learning styles inventory; free conversation analysis; no explicit profiling prompt | A quiz reveals knowledge level but not encoding architecture. Free conversation analysis requires extensive data. A single teaching episode reveals channel preference (do they tell stories, draw diagrams, build logical chains?), emotional trigger (what makes their teaching come alive?), and challenge calibration (how much complexity do they naturally introduce?). One prompt, four dimensions. |
| 7 | **Content type before learner profile (structure before delivery)** | Craik & Lockhart (1972): the nature of the material constrains what kinds of processing are possible. You cannot process the semantic meaning of a random number sequence -- it has no semantic content. Procedural knowledge (how to) requires different encoding structure than conceptual knowledge (why). Mayer (2009): the structure of content determines which multimedia principles apply. | Learner profile first, then adapt content; simultaneous assessment; no content typing | Wrong structure with right delivery still fails. Teaching procedural knowledge (coding, cooking) through narrative without steps will fail regardless of how well the narrative matches the learner's primary channel. Teaching systemic knowledge (ecosystems, markets) through isolated facts will fail regardless of how well the facts are spaced. The content determines the architecture. The learner determines the delivery within that architecture. |
| 8 | **Emotional encoding is mandatory, not optional** | McGaugh (2000, 2004): the amygdala-hippocampus consolidation pathway is not a supplementary system -- it is the primary mechanism for modulating memory storage strength. Memory consolidation occurs during sleep and post-encoding rest; the amygdala's noradrenergic modulation during initial encoding determines which memories receive enhanced consolidation. | Emotional framing as optional enhancement; emotional framing only for difficult content; no emotional framing (purely cognitive approach) | The neuroscience leaves no room for "optional." The amygdala-hippocampus pathway is how mammalian memory consolidation works. Information encoded without emotional engagement is information encoded with baseline consolidation. The question is not whether to engage the pathway but how to do so appropriately -- matching the emotional carrier to the learner's trigger type ensures the engagement serves the learning rather than distracting from it. |
| 9 | **Dual channel MINIMUM, not dual channel "when possible"** | Paivio (1986, 1991): two independent encoding systems (verbal + nonverbal) create two independent retrieval paths. Mayer (2009): the multimedia principle is one of the most robust findings in instructional research -- people learn better from words + pictures than words alone. The effect is not marginal; it is consistent and large across hundreds of experiments. | Dual channel as recommendation; single channel with optional secondary; adaptive (sometimes dual, sometimes single) | "When possible" becomes "when convenient," which becomes "rarely." Single-channel encoding creates a single point of failure -- if the verbal trace degrades, the information is lost. Dual coding creates redundancy. Making it a minimum, not a recommendation, ensures that every key concept has at least two retrieval paths. The cost of adding a secondary channel is marginal; the benefit to retention is substantial. |
| 10 | **The encoding stack has six levels with those specific percentages** | The six levels map to empirically documented processing depths. Receive (20%): Craik & Lockhart -- shallow processing produces rapid forgetting; lecture retention studies show ~5-20% retention of passively received material. Retrieve (55%): Roediger & Karpicke (2006) -- tested group retained 56% vs. 42% for restudied group. Apply (65%): transfer studies show applied knowledge retained better than merely recalled knowledge. Simulate (75%): Schacter & Addis (2007) -- constructive simulation engages the same hippocampal system as memory, creating rich cross-linked traces. Connect (85%): elaborative encoding research shows schema integration produces highly durable traces. Teach (100%): the protege effect (Fiorella & Mayer, 2013) -- preparing to teach and teaching produce the deepest encoding measured. | Bloom's Taxonomy (six levels); three levels (passive/active/generative); continuous rather than discrete | The specific percentages are estimates calibrated to the research, not precise measurements. They serve as design targets, not scientific claims. Bloom's Taxonomy addresses cognitive complexity, not encoding depth -- a high-Bloom analysis task can be performed shallowly. The encoding stack measures depth of encoding specifically, which is what determines retention. See ENCODING-STACK.md for detailed evidence for each level. |
| 11 | **Apply (Layer 3) is the minimum acceptable level, not Retrieve (Layer 2)** | Karpicke & Blunt (2011): retrieval practice beats restudying, but application (using retrieved knowledge to solve novel problems) produces even better transfer. Roediger & Karpicke (2006): retrieval alone improves verbatim retention but the spec targets usable knowledge, not just recallable facts. Application requires both retrieval AND deployment, engaging deeper processing. | Retrieve as minimum (easier to achieve); Simulate as minimum (higher quality but harder to create); no minimum (let context determine) | Retrieve (Layer 2) ensures the learner can recall information -- but recall without application is trivia. The specification targets usable knowledge: information that can be deployed in novel contexts. Apply (Layer 3) is the lowest level that requires the learner to DO something with what they've learned, not just recall it. This is achievable in every interaction (even a brief one can include "try this") while producing meaningfully deeper encoding than retrieval alone. |
| 12 | **Retrieval is woven into flow instead of separate practice sessions** | Ebbinghaus (1885): the forgetting curve begins immediately after encoding -- waiting for a separate practice session means fighting a steeper curve. Cepeda et al. (2006): the spacing effect works with any inter-study interval, including very short ones. Bjork & Bjork (2011): retrieval practice is most effective when it occurs in context, where the retrieved knowledge can connect to new information. | Separate spaced repetition system (Anki-style); end-of-session review; scheduled follow-up sessions | Separate practice sessions create a structural barrier: the learner must open a different tool, context-switch, and perform isolated recall. Woven retrieval happens in the flow of conversation: "Remember when we discussed X? Same principle applies here." The callback references earlier material while advancing new material, producing both retrieval and elaboration simultaneously. No context switch. No separate tool. The spacing happens inside the learning. |
| 13 | **Comprehension pulses use "1-5" scale, not "correct/incorrect"** | Bjork (1994): performance during learning is a poor index of long-term learning. Binary correct/incorrect measures current performance, which the research shows is unreliable for predicting retention. A 1-5 subjective confidence scale measures metacognitive state -- how solid the learner feels about the material -- which, while imperfect, provides calibration information that binary measures miss. | Binary correct/incorrect; 1-10 scale; free-form response; no explicit checking | Binary correct/incorrect turns learning into testing. It activates evaluation anxiety, changes the social dynamic, and provides less information (a "correct" answer may reflect shallow pattern-matching or deep understanding -- the binary cannot distinguish). A 1-5 confidence scale is explicitly framed as "not a test" -- it is a temperature read. It tells the system whether to slow down (1-2), stay on track (3), or speed up (4-5). The scale is small enough to be answered without deliberation. |
| 14 | **The profiler only runs once (unless recalibrated)** | Learning profiles are relatively stable cognitive architectures, not session-by-session states. Paivio (1986): the relative strength of verbal vs. imagery processing is a stable individual difference. Channel preferences, emotional triggers, and challenge tolerance are trait-like characteristics that do not shift between conversations. | Re-profile every session; continuous background profiling; profile decay over time | Re-profiling every session wastes the opening of each interaction on assessment rather than learning. Continuous background profiling would require the system to second-guess its own assessment, creating instability. The profile is set once and then calibrated within sessions through comprehension pulses and engagement signals. If the profile is wrong, the user can explicitly request recalibration. |
| 15 | **Six output layers mapped to the seven principles** | Each layer makes a specific learning mechanism operational: Channel Adaptation (Principle 4), Emotional Hooks (Principle 3), Difficulty Gates (Principle 6), Generation Prompts (Principle 5), Callback Threads (Principle 7), Comprehension Pulses (Principle 6). Principles 1 and 2 (Detect, Content Before Learner) operate at the assessment level, not the output level -- they shape what the output layers do, not how they appear. | One layer per principle (seven layers); three layers (adapt/encode/retrieve); no structured layers | Six layers balance operational visibility with usability. Seven layers would require separate output for detection and content typing, which are internal operations the learner never needs to see. Three layers would lose the ability to trace specific mechanisms. Six layers mean every visible element in a response can be identified with the specific learning mechanism it implements. |
| 16 | **"Every element pays rent"** | Mayer's (2009) coherence principle: people learn better when extraneous material is excluded. Cognitive load theory (Sweller, 1988): working memory has finite capacity; every extraneous element consumes capacity that could serve learning. | Allow decorative elements for engagement; fixed density of learning elements per response; adaptive density based on content type | Decorative learning elements are worse than absent ones. A generation prompt that doesn't serve the current learning creates interruption without benefit. A callback reference to material that isn't relevant creates confusion. An emotional hook that doesn't connect to the concept being learned is manipulation. Every element must earn its place by directly serving the encoding of the current material. |
| 17 | **Difficulty gates are "Before reading on -- what do you think?" not quizzes** | Slamecka & Graf (1978): the generation effect -- generating an answer before receiving it produces better encoding of the subsequent information, even if the generated answer is wrong. Bjork & Bjork (2011): the "pretesting effect" -- attempting to answer before instruction improves learning from the subsequent instruction. | Post-instruction quizzes; multiple-choice checkpoints; no gates (continuous flow) | Pre-instruction generation activates the retrieval and prediction systems BEFORE the information arrives, creating a "slot" for the incoming information to fill. This produces deeper encoding than post-instruction testing (which also works but misses the priming benefit). The conversational framing ("what do you think?") avoids the evaluative anxiety of a quiz while activating the same generation mechanism. |
| 18 | **Composite profiles are "starting points, not cages"** | No two learners are identical. Paivio (1991): individual differences in verbal and imagery processing are continuous, not categorical. The five profiles (Explorer, Architect, Storyteller, Builder, Pattern-Finder) are attractors in a continuous space, not discrete types. | Fixed types with rigid rules; no types (fully continuous); self-selected types | Types are useful as starting points because they provide immediate design guidance -- knowing someone is an Explorer gives the system a working hypothesis about channel preference, emotional trigger, and challenge level. But treating types as fixed categories would reproduce the same error as learning styles: categorizing individuals and then delivering to the category rather than the individual. The specification explicitly marks profiles as starting points that should be adapted fluidly based on ongoing observation. |

## Integration Decisions

| # | Decision | Evidence | Alternatives Considered | Rationale |
|---|----------|----------|------------------------|-----------|
| 19 | **Learn Mode does not auto-disable for "simple" tasks** | Retrieval science applies to all information, not just complex information. A simple fact benefits from dual coding and spaced retrieval just as much as a complex concept. Ebbinghaus (1885) used nonsense syllables -- the simplest possible material -- and found the same encoding/retrieval principles apply. | Disable for simple lookups; reduce dial automatically for routine tasks; active only for "learning sessions" | The research does not support a distinction between "information worth encoding" and "information not worth encoding." A phone number benefits from dual coding. A name benefits from emotional association. The dial allows the user to reduce intensity for routine tasks, but the system does not make that judgment -- because the system cannot know which information the user will need to recall later. |
| 20 | **Layers from different axes coexist without conflict** | The five research programs describe independent cognitive dimensions. Memory encoding (learn) is orthogonal to perceptual processing (creative mode). Retrieval mechanisms (learn) are orthogonal to generative mechanisms (spark mode). A Callback Thread can operate inside a creative tangent because encoding and perception are different cognitive operations. | Merge layers across axes; prioritize learn layers over others; disable learn for creative/spark tasks | The research supports orthogonality. Paivio's dual coding does not compete with creative perception -- it operates on a different cognitive dimension. A creative insight benefits from dual-channel encoding just as much as a factual explanation. Merging or prioritizing would violate the independence the research describes. |
