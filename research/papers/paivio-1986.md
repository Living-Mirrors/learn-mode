# Mental Representations: A Dual Coding Approach

**Author(s):** Allan Paivio (University of Western Ontario)
**Published:** Oxford University Press, 1986
**ISBN:** 978-0195066661
**Field:** Cognitive Psychology / Memory

---

## Abstract

Paivio presents the definitive formulation of dual coding theory, proposing that human cognition operates through two independent but interconnected coding systems: a verbal system that processes and stores linguistic information (logogens), and a nonverbal system that processes and stores imagery and sensory information (imagens). The two systems are structurally distinct -- they encode different types of information, use different representational units, and can operate independently. However, they are also interconnected through referential links: a word can activate an image, and an image can activate a word. Information encoded in both systems simultaneously -- dual coded information -- is more robustly stored and more easily retrieved than information encoded in only one system, because it has two independent retrieval paths.

## Key Findings

- **Two independent coding systems.** The verbal system (logogens) processes language sequentially. The nonverbal system (imagens) processes imagery synchronously. They are not two aspects of a single system -- they are structurally and functionally distinct. Evidence: selective interference paradigms show that verbal and imagery tasks compete for resources within their own system but not across systems.

- **Dual coded information is remembered better.** Concrete words (which naturally evoke images) are remembered significantly better than abstract words (which primarily engage the verbal system). This "concreteness effect" is one of the most reliable findings in memory research. The mechanism is not that concrete words are "easier" -- it is that they activate both coding systems, creating two independent memory traces.

- **The additivity hypothesis.** The memory advantage of dual coding is additive, not multiplicative. Two traces are approximately twice as likely to be retrieved as one trace, because each provides an independent retrieval path. If the verbal trace is degraded, the imagery trace may still be intact (and vice versa).

- **This is NOT learning styles.** Dual coding theory makes no claim that some people are "visual learners" and others are "verbal learners." All humans have both systems. The theory claims that encoding in both systems produces better memory than encoding in one -- for everyone. Individual differences exist in the relative strength of the two systems, but the dual coding advantage is universal.

## Methodology

The book synthesizes decades of experimental research using: (a) paired-associate learning experiments comparing concrete and abstract word pairs; (b) selective interference paradigms (Brooks, 1968) demonstrating independence of verbal and imagery processing; (c) free recall experiments manipulating imageability of stimulus materials; (d) recognition memory experiments comparing single-coded and dual-coded items; (e) developmental studies showing the emergence of verbal and imagery coding across childhood.

## Relevance to Learn Mode

**Principle:** #4 -- Dual Channel Minimum
**Specific connection:** Dual coding theory is the direct theoretical foundation for Principle 4. The specification's requirement that all key information be encoded in at least two channels is a direct implementation of Paivio's finding that dual-coded information has two independent retrieval paths. The specification's channel adaptation system (reshaping information to the learner's primary and secondary channels) operationalizes the referential connections between coding systems -- ensuring that both the verbal and nonverbal systems are engaged for every key concept.
**What this book provides that others don't:** The theoretical architecture. Mayer's multimedia learning (2009) provides the instructional design principles, but Paivio provides the cognitive model that explains WHY dual-channel encoding works. The two systems, the referential links, the additivity hypothesis -- this is the foundation on which the specification's channel adaptation rests.

## Key Quotes

> "Dual coding theory assumes that there are two cognitive subsystems, one specialized for the representation and processing of nonverbal objects and events, the other specialized for dealing with language."

> "The memory advantage of concrete over abstract words is one of the most robust findings in the experimental study of memory. It reflects the availability of two codes -- imaginal and verbal -- for concrete stimuli, as compared with one code for abstract stimuli."

> "The independence assumption is critical: the two systems can be active simultaneously, and the activity of one does not require the activity of the other. When both are active, the resulting memory representation has two retrieval routes rather than one."

## Limitations

Dual coding theory was developed primarily from studies using isolated words and simple paired associates. Its application to complex, multi-sentence learning materials is theoretically motivated but less directly tested in the original work. The theory also does not specify how the two systems handle different types of imagery (spatial, kinesthetic, auditory) -- the nonverbal system is treated as a single system, though later work (Mayer, 2009) distinguishes visual from auditory channels more precisely. The learning styles movement inappropriately co-opted dual coding language to support the idea of "visual" vs. "auditory" learners -- a claim the theory does not make and the evidence does not support.
