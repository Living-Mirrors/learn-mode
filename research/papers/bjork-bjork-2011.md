# Making Things Hard on Yourself, But in a Good Way: Creating Desirable Difficulties to Enhance Learning

**Author(s):** Robert A. Bjork (UCLA), Elizabeth L. Bjork (UCLA)
**Published:** In M. A. Gernsbacher, R. W. Pew, L. M. Hough, & J. R. Pomerantz (Eds.), *Psychology and the Real World* (pp. 56-64). Worth Publishers, 2011
**Field:** Cognitive Psychology / Learning Science

---

## Abstract

Bjork and Bjork introduce the concept of desirable difficulties -- conditions during learning that appear to slow acquisition and increase errors but actually enhance long-term retention and transfer. Four classes of desirable difficulties are identified: spacing (distributing practice over time), interleaving (mixing different categories of problems), generation (producing answers rather than reading them), and variation (changing the conditions of practice). The chapter also introduces the critical distinction between learning and performance: current performance during training is a poor predictor of long-term learning. Conditions that produce fast, fluent performance often produce rapid forgetting, while conditions that produce slow, effortful performance often produce durable retention.

## Key Findings

- **Spacing.** Distributing practice over time produces dramatically better long-term retention than massing practice into a single session. The effect is robust across materials, populations, and paradigms. Massed practice feels more productive (the material seems familiar during the second study session), but distributed practice produces better retention on delayed tests.

- **Interleaving.** Mixing different categories during practice (e.g., alternating between types of math problems rather than blocking by type) slows acquisition but improves the ability to discriminate between categories and select appropriate strategies on later tests. The mechanism: interleaving forces the learner to identify WHICH strategy applies, not just execute the strategy.

- **Generation.** Producing information (generating an answer, completing a fragment, solving a problem) produces better learning than reading the same information, even when the generation attempt is unsuccessful. The effort of generation creates a richer encoding than passive reception.

- **Variation.** Changing the conditions of practice (different examples, different contexts, different surface features) produces better transfer than practicing under constant conditions. The mechanism: variation forces the learner to extract the underlying structure rather than relying on surface cues.

- **The illusion of learning.** The most dangerous finding: conditions that produce fast, fluent performance during learning create a subjective sense of mastery that is systematically misleading. Massed practice feels effective. Re-reading feels productive. Highlighting feels active. All produce worse long-term retention than their more effortful alternatives. The subjective experience of learning is a poor guide to actual learning.

## Methodology

This is a concept paper and review chapter, synthesizing decades of experimental research from the Bjorks' laboratory and the broader learning science community. The evidence comes from controlled experiments comparing: massed vs. distributed practice, blocked vs. interleaved practice, reading vs. generating, constant vs. varied practice. The dependent variables are long-term retention (delayed tests, typically 1 day to 1 week after final practice) and transfer (performance on novel problems or in novel contexts).

## Relevance to Learn Mode

**Principle:** #6 -- Calibrate in Real-Time
**Specific connection:** Desirable difficulties are the theoretical foundation for Principle 6. The specification's difficulty calibration system must navigate a paradox: the conditions that feel like learning (easy, fluent, fast) are often the conditions that produce the least learning, while the conditions that feel difficult (slow, effortful, error-prone) often produce the most learning. The specification cannot simply ask the learner "is this too hard?" because the learner's subjective assessment is unreliable (the illusion of learning). Instead, it uses comprehension pulses, engagement signals, and behavioral observation to maintain the learner in the zone of desirable difficulty -- challenged enough that effortful processing is occurring, but not so overwhelmed that processing breaks down.
**What this chapter provides that others don't:** The unified framework. Individual desirable difficulties (spacing, testing, generation) were documented in separate research programs. The Bjorks synthesize them into a single concept with a single mechanism: conditions that force effortful processing enhance encoding. The chapter also names the enemy: the illusion of learning, the subjective sense of mastery that fluent processing produces. This is the problem Learn Mode must solve.

## Key Quotes

> "Conditions of learning that make performance improve rapidly often fail to support long-term retention and transfer, whereas conditions that create challenges and slow the rate of apparent learning often optimize long-term retention and transfer."

> "The most important implication of the distinction between learning and performance is this: we cannot, as learners or as instructors, rely on our subjective experience during learning as an accurate index of whether learning is actually happening."

> "Desirable difficulties, unlike the name might suggest, are desirable because they trigger encoding and retrieval processes that support learning, comprehension, and remembering. If, however, the learner does not have the background knowledge or skills to respond to them successfully, they become undesirable difficulties."

## Limitations

The concept paper does not provide precise calibration guidelines for when difficulties become undesirable. The principle that difficulties enhance learning "when the learner has the background knowledge to respond" leaves the boundary undefined. In practice, this boundary must be discovered empirically for each learner -- which is exactly what Learn Mode's real-time calibration (Principle 6) is designed to do, but the research does not provide the algorithm. The chapter also focuses on laboratory learning with well-defined materials; the application to unstructured, conversational learning (the primary context for AI) requires significant design inference.
