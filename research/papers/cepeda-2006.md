# Distributed Practice in Verbal Recall Tasks: A Review and Quantitative Synthesis

**Author(s):** Nicholas J. Cepeda (York University), Harold Pashler (UC San Diego), Edward Vul (MIT), John T. Wixted (UC San Diego), Doug Rohrer (University of South Florida)
**Published:** *Psychological Bulletin*, 132(3), 354-380, 2006
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.132.3.354
**Field:** Cognitive Psychology / Learning Science

---

## Abstract

Cepeda et al. conducted a meta-analysis of 184 articles encompassing 317 experiments on the spacing effect in verbal recall tasks. The review spans the entire history of spacing research from Ebbinghaus (1885) to contemporary studies. The central finding is unambiguous: distributing practice over time reliably improves long-term retention compared to massing practice, across virtually all conditions studied. The effect is robust across different types of material (words, sentences, text passages), different retention intervals (minutes to months), different age groups (children to older adults), and different methodologies. The spacing effect is one of the most reliable phenomena in all of experimental psychology.

## Key Findings

- **The spacing effect is universal.** Of the 317 experiments reviewed, the vast majority found a distributed practice advantage. The effect held across verbal materials of all types, retention intervals ranging from minutes to months, and populations including children, young adults, and older adults. No moderator variable was identified that reverses the effect -- distributed practice was never significantly worse than massed practice under any condition.

- **Longer spacing intervals produce better long-term retention (up to a point).** As the interval between practice sessions increases, long-term retention improves -- but only up to an optimal point, beyond which further spacing reduces retention (because the material is forgotten between sessions). The optimal spacing interval is a function of the desired retention interval: if you want to remember for a week, space sessions by 1-2 days; if you want to remember for a year, space sessions by weeks.

- **The optimal spacing-to-retention ratio is approximately 10-30%.** The optimal inter-study interval is roughly 10-30% of the desired retention interval. Want to retain for 10 days? Space by 1-3 days. Want to retain for a year? Space by 5-10 weeks. This is an approximation -- the exact optimum varies by material and learner -- but the ratio provides practical design guidance.

- **Effect sizes are large and consistent.** The spacing advantage is not a small statistical artifact. Effect sizes across the meta-analysis were consistently in the medium-to-large range, comparable to or exceeding the effect sizes of many instructional interventions that require far more effort and resources.

## Methodology

This is a quantitative meta-analysis of 184 articles containing 317 experiments on the spacing effect in verbal recall tasks. The analysis includes studies from 1885 (Ebbinghaus) through 2004. Inclusion criteria: (a) at least one massed and one distributed practice condition; (b) a retention test administered after a delay; (c) verbal material (words, word pairs, sentences, text passages, vocabulary); (d) sufficient statistical information to compute effect sizes. The analysis examined moderator variables including type of material, retention interval, spacing interval, type of test (recall vs. recognition), and age of participants.

## Relevance to Learn Mode

**Principle:** #7 -- Weave Retrieval Into Flow
**Specific connection:** Cepeda et al.'s meta-analysis provides the quantitative confirmation that the spacing effect -- Ebbinghaus's second great discovery -- is not a fragile laboratory phenomenon but one of the most robust findings in psychology. The specification's callback threads and woven retrieval are direct implementations of the spacing effect. The practical implication is that Learn Mode does not need a separate spaced repetition system (Anki, SuperMemo). Instead, spaced retrieval can be woven into the natural flow of conversation: referencing earlier material at increasing intervals creates the distributed practice that the meta-analysis confirms enhances retention. The 10-30% ratio provides practical guidance for callback timing.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** The quantitative synthesis. Ebbinghaus (1885) discovered the spacing effect. Bjork and Bjork (2011) explained why it works. Cepeda et al. (2006) demonstrated how large and how reliable it is, across 121 years of research. This is the "how confident should we be?" paper -- and the answer is "extremely."

## Key Quotes

> "The distributed practice effect is one of the most reliable and robust findings in experimental psychology. Our review of 317 experiments, spanning 120 years, finds no conditions under which massed practice reliably outperforms distributed practice for long-term retention."

> "The optimal spacing interval increases with the desired retention interval. For practical purposes, the ratio of spacing interval to retention interval should fall in the range of approximately 10-30%."

> "The effect sizes associated with distributed practice are comparable to or larger than many educational interventions that have received far more attention in policy discussions."

## Limitations

The meta-analysis is restricted to verbal recall tasks. The spacing effect has been documented in motor learning, perceptual learning, and other domains, but this review does not cover those literatures. The optimal spacing-to-retention ratio is an approximation based on aggregate data -- individual variation around this optimum is not well-characterized. The review also does not address how to implement spaced practice in conversational or AI-mediated contexts, where the timing of retrieval events is constrained by the flow of interaction rather than a predetermined schedule.
