# Uber das Gedachtnis (Memory: A Contribution to Experimental Psychology)

**Author(s):** Hermann Ebbinghaus (University of Berlin)
**Published:** 1885 (English translation: Teachers College, Columbia University, 1913)
**Field:** Experimental Psychology / Memory

---

## Abstract

Ebbinghaus conducted the first systematic experimental study of human memory, using himself as the sole subject and nonsense syllables (consonant-vowel-consonant trigrams like DAX, BUP, ZOL) as stimuli to eliminate the confounding effects of prior associations. Over a series of experiments spanning two years, he established two foundational findings that have been replicated continuously for 140 years: the forgetting curve (memory decays as a negative exponential function of time, with the steepest decline occurring in the first hours after learning) and the spacing effect (distributing learning sessions over time produces dramatically better retention than concentrating them into a single session, even when total study time is identical).

## Key Findings

- **The forgetting curve.** Ebbinghaus measured retention of nonsense syllable lists at intervals ranging from 20 minutes to 31 days. He found that forgetting follows a negative exponential function: approximately 40% of material was forgotten within 20 minutes, 55% within one hour, 65% within one day, and 75% within one week. The curve flattens after the initial steep decline -- what remains after several days tends to be relatively stable. This mathematical form has been confirmed across every material type, sensory modality, and population tested since 1885.

- **The spacing effect.** Ebbinghaus discovered that distributing learning sessions across days produced significantly better retention than massing the same number of repetitions into a single session. This was true even when total study time was held constant. Distributed practice consistently required fewer total repetitions to reach a criterion level of retention than massed practice -- learning was literally more efficient when spaced.

- **Savings method.** Ebbinghaus invented the "savings" method for measuring memory: the number of repetitions required to relearn a previously learned list, expressed as a percentage of the original learning. Even when recall appeared to be zero (the subject could not consciously remember any items from the list), relearning was faster than original learning -- demonstrating that memory traces persist below the threshold of conscious recall.

- **Serial position effects.** Ebbinghaus noted that items at the beginning and end of lists were learned and retained more easily than items in the middle -- the serial position curve. This finding would later be theoretically important for distinguishing short-term and long-term memory systems.

## Methodology

Ebbinghaus used himself as the sole subject -- a significant methodological limitation but one that allowed extraordinary experimental control. He generated lists of 13 nonsense syllables, learned each list to a criterion of two perfect recitations, and then measured retention at various intervals using the savings method. He controlled for time of day, fatigue, and mood by conducting sessions at the same time each day under similar conditions. Over two years (1879-1880 and 1883-1884), he completed hundreds of experimental sessions.

## Relevance to Learn Mode

**Principle:** #7 -- Weave Retrieval Into Flow
**Specific connection:** Ebbinghaus established the two phenomena that Principle 7 is designed to address. The forgetting curve shows that memory decays rapidly after encoding -- without intervention, most information is lost within days. The spacing effect shows that distributed retrieval events can defeat this curve. Learn Mode's callback threads ("Remember when we discussed X?") are designed to create spaced retrieval events within the natural flow of conversation, defeating the forgetting curve without requiring the learner to use a separate spaced repetition system. Every callback is a retrieval event; every retrieval event resets the forgetting curve for that piece of information.
**What this work provides that others don't:** The absolute foundation. Every finding in the Learn Mode research archive ultimately rests on Ebbinghaus's two discoveries: memory fades without intervention, and spaced retrieval defeats that fading. The forgetting curve is the problem. The spacing effect is the solution. Everything else -- testing effects, generation effects, desirable difficulties -- builds on this foundation.

## Key Quotes

> "With any considerable number of repetitions a suitable distribution of them over a space of time is decidedly more advantageous than the massing of them at a single time." (Ebbinghaus, 1885/1913, p. 89)

> "The rapidity of forgetting is at first very great, and then gradually lessens with the lapse of time."

> "Left to itself every mental content gradually loses its capacity for being revived, or at least suffers loss in this regard under the influence of time."

## Limitations

The most obvious limitation is N=1 -- Ebbinghaus was his own sole subject. The findings might reflect idiosyncrasies of his own memory system. However, the forgetting curve and spacing effect have been replicated in thousands of subsequent studies with diverse populations, making the single-subject limitation historically significant but empirically inconsequential. The use of nonsense syllables, while providing experimental control, raises questions about ecological validity -- does the forgetting curve look the same for meaningful material? Subsequent research confirms that the mathematical form is similar but the rate of forgetting is modulated by meaningfulness, emotional engagement, and depth of encoding. Finally, Ebbinghaus had no theory of WHY the spacing effect works -- the mechanistic explanation (retrieval as encoding, desirable difficulty) came a century later.
