# Dual Coding Theory: Retrospect and Current Status

**Author(s):** Allan Paivio (University of Western Ontario)
**Published:** *Canadian Journal of Psychology*, 45(3), 255-287, 1991
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1037/h0084295
**Field:** Cognitive Psychology / Memory

---

## Abstract

Paivio reviews the first 25 years of dual coding theory, responding to theoretical challenges from propositional (amodal) accounts of memory representation. The propositional view holds that all knowledge is stored in a single, abstract, language-like format -- not in modality-specific codes. Paivio marshals evidence from behavioral experiments, neuropsychological case studies, and neuroimaging to defend the claim that verbal and nonverbal representations are structurally distinct systems, not surface manifestations of a single propositional code. He updates the evidence base with findings from the intervening decades and addresses criticisms regarding the scope, testability, and parsimony of dual coding theory.

## Key Findings

- **Against propositional alternatives.** The propositional hypothesis (Pylyshyn, 1973; Anderson, 1978) claims that all memory representations are amodal propositions -- abstract, language-like structures that are neither verbal nor imagistic. Paivio argues this fails to account for: (a) the robust concreteness effect (concrete words are remembered better); (b) selective interference (verbal tasks interfere with verbal tasks but not imagery tasks); (c) neuropsychological dissociations (patients can lose verbal ability while retaining imagery, or vice versa).

- **Neuropsychological evidence.** Double dissociations between verbal and imagery processing in brain-damaged patients provide the strongest evidence for two systems. Patients with left hemisphere damage can lose verbal processing while retaining imagery. Patients with right hemisphere damage can lose imagery while retaining verbal processing. A single propositional system cannot explain these dissociations.

- **Updated concreteness data.** Twenty-five years of accumulated evidence confirmed that the concreteness effect is robust across: free recall, cued recall, recognition, serial learning, and paired-associate learning. The effect generalizes across languages, age groups, and experimental paradigms.

- **Individual differences are real but secondary.** People do differ in their relative strength of verbal vs. imagery processing (measured by questionnaires like the QMI and by behavioral tests). However, the dual coding advantage -- that encoding in both systems produces better memory -- holds across the entire range of individual differences. High imagers benefit from dual coding. High verbalizers benefit from dual coding. The advantage is universal; the relative channel strength varies.

## Methodology

This is a comprehensive theoretical review and defense, synthesizing evidence from: (a) behavioral experiments on the concreteness effect in memory; (b) selective interference studies; (c) neuropsychological case studies of patients with focal brain damage; (d) developmental studies; (e) cross-linguistic studies of the concreteness effect. The paper responds point-by-point to criticisms from propositional theorists.

## Relevance to Learn Mode

**Principle:** #4 -- Dual Channel Minimum
**Specific connection:** This paper provides the theoretical defense for why dual coding is a structural feature of human memory, not a design preference. The propositional alternative -- if correct -- would mean that how information is represented does not matter as long as the abstract content is preserved. Paivio's defense demonstrates that representational format matters fundamentally. Information encoded as both words and images is stored in two neurologically distinct systems with two independent retrieval paths. This is not a pedagogical technique -- it is a property of neural architecture. The specification's requirement for dual channel MINIMUM (not "when convenient") is grounded in this evidence: single-channel encoding is structurally weaker regardless of content quality.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** The theoretical defense against the strongest counterargument. Anyone encountering dual coding theory will eventually encounter the propositional critique ("isn't it all really one system?"). This paper provides the empirical rebuttal, grounded in neuropsychological evidence that a single-system account cannot explain.

## Key Quotes

> "The weight of evidence from behavioral experiments, neuropsychological case studies, and neuroimaging now overwhelmingly favors the dual coding position: verbal and nonverbal representations are functionally and neurologically distinct systems, not surface manifestations of a deeper propositional code."

> "Individual differences in imagery and verbal ability are real and measurable. But they do not invalidate the dual coding principle. High verbalizers still benefit from imagery encoding. High imagers still benefit from verbal encoding. The dual coding advantage transcends individual differences in channel strength."

> "The concreteness effect is not a curiosity confined to laboratory word lists. It extends to sentences, to paragraphs, to instructional texts. Concrete, imageable language consistently produces better memory than abstract language -- a finding no propositional theory has adequately explained."

## Limitations

This is a retrospective review and defense, not a new empirical contribution. The evidence marshaled is drawn from existing studies and reinterpreted within the dual coding framework. The paper does not address how dual coding operates in naturalistic learning environments (classrooms, conversations, on-the-job training) -- the evidence base is primarily laboratory experiments with word lists and simple paired associates. The paper also does not address the emerging multimedia learning literature (Mayer's program began in the early 1990s), which would later provide the instructional bridge between dual coding theory and practical application.
