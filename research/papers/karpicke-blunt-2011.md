# Retrieval Practice Produces More Learning than Elaborative Studying with Concept Mapping

**Author(s):** Jeffrey D. Karpicke (Purdue University), Janell R. Blunt (Purdue University)
**Published:** *Science*, 331(6018), 772-775, 2011
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1199327
**Field:** Cognitive Psychology / Learning Science

---

## Abstract

Karpicke and Blunt directly compare retrieval practice to concept mapping -- a widely recommended "active learning" strategy that involves creating visual diagrams of relationships among concepts. In two experiments, students studied science texts and then either practiced retrieval (free recall of the material) or created elaborate concept maps. On a final test one week later, the retrieval practice group significantly outperformed the concept mapping group on both verbatim questions (direct recall of facts) and inference questions (requiring application of knowledge to new situations). The finding is striking because concept mapping is often presented as the gold standard of active learning, yet retrieval practice -- a simpler, less elaborative activity -- produced more learning.

## Key Findings

- **Retrieval practice beat concept mapping on verbatim questions.** One week after learning, students who practiced retrieval recalled more specific facts from the texts than students who created concept maps. This held despite the concept mapping group spending more time actively engaging with the material in an ostensibly deeper way.

- **Retrieval practice beat concept mapping on inference questions.** The critical test: inference questions required students to apply the learned concepts to novel situations -- to draw conclusions that were not explicitly stated in the texts. Retrieval practice produced better performance on these transfer questions as well. This means retrieval practice does not merely enhance rote recall -- it enhances the kind of flexible, applicable knowledge that education values most.

- **Students predicted the opposite.** When asked to predict their future performance, students expected concept mapping to produce better results than retrieval practice. As in Roediger and Karpicke (2006), learners' metacognitive judgments were systematically wrong -- they preferred the strategy that felt more productive but produced less learning.

- **The mechanism: the struggle to retrieve IS the learning.** Concept mapping involves elaboration -- creating visual relationships, organizing information, labeling connections. These are genuinely active processes. But they do not require retrieval -- the text is present during mapping. Retrieval practice requires the learner to search memory, reconstruct the information, and produce it without external support. This retrieval effort is what creates the encoding benefit. Elaboration without retrieval is less effective than retrieval without elaboration.

## Methodology

Two experiments. In Experiment 1, students studied a science text about sea otters and then either practiced retrieval (free recall on a blank sheet) or created a concept map (with the text available for reference). One week later, both groups took a short-answer test with verbatim and inference questions. In Experiment 2, the design was replicated with additional texts and with the concept mapping group creating maps from memory (without the text) to equalize retrieval demands. Even under these conditions, retrieval practice produced equivalent or better performance.

## Relevance to Learn Mode

**Principle:** #5 -- Push Up the Stack
**Specific connection:** This paper closes a critical gap in the argument for the encoding stack. Concept mapping is an "active learning" strategy that many educators consider superior to simple recall. If concept mapping were consistently better, the encoding stack would need a different structure -- elaborative activities at every level. Karpicke and Blunt show that it is not the elaborateness of the activity that matters but whether it requires retrieval. This validates the encoding stack's architecture: each level above Receive requires progressively MORE production, and it is the production (not the elaboration) that drives encoding depth. Apply (Layer 3), Simulate (Layer 4), Connect (Layer 5), and Teach (Layer 6) are all effective not because they are elaborate but because they require the learner to produce -- to retrieve, reconstruct, and generate.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** The head-to-head comparison with the strongest alternative. If retrieval practice only beat passive restudying, critics could argue that any active strategy would do as well. Karpicke and Blunt show that retrieval practice beats concept mapping -- one of the most effortful, elaborative strategies available. The implication is that the mechanism is specifically retrieval/production, not merely "being active."

## Key Quotes

> "Practicing retrieval produces greater gains in meaningful learning than elaborative studying with concept mapping."

> "The critical factor is not whether the learner is actively engaged with the material -- concept mapping is undeniably active. The critical factor is whether the learner is required to retrieve the material from memory."

> "Students predicted that concept mapping would produce better long-term learning than retrieval practice. Their predictions were wrong, replicating the finding that learners are poor judges of which strategies produce durable learning."

## Limitations

The study used science texts as learning materials. While the finding generalizes across the two experiments, replication with other content domains (mathematics, history, skill learning) would strengthen the case. The concept maps were created in a single session -- extended concept mapping over multiple sessions might show different results. The study also does not address whether combining retrieval practice with concept mapping produces better results than either alone -- it is possible that retrieval practice followed by elaborative organization is superior to either in isolation. The specification's encoding stack suggests this implicitly: higher levels (Connect, Teach) combine retrieval with elaboration.
