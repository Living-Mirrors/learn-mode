# Test-Enhanced Learning: Taking Memory Tests Improves Long-Term Retention

**Author(s):** Henry L. Roediger III (Washington University in St. Louis), Jeffrey D. Karpicke (Purdue University)
**Published:** *Psychological Science*, 17(3), 249-255, 2006
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2006.01693.x
**Field:** Cognitive Psychology / Learning Science

---

## Abstract

Roediger and Karpicke demonstrate the testing effect in two experiments: taking a memory test on previously studied material produces MORE long-term learning than spending the same amount of time restudying the material. In Experiment 1, participants studied prose passages and either restudied them or took a free recall test. After 5 minutes, the restudy group performed slightly better. After 2 days, the testing group performed slightly better. After 1 week, the testing group dramatically outperformed the restudy group (56% vs. 42%). In Experiment 2, repeated testing (STTT) was compared to repeated studying (SSSS) and mixed conditions. The results confirmed: more testing, less studying = better long-term retention.

## Key Findings

- **The testing effect reverses over time.** At short retention intervals (5 minutes), restudying produces equal or slightly better performance than testing -- the material is still fresh. At longer intervals (2 days, 1 week), testing produces significantly better retention. This crossover interaction is critical: it means that the testing advantage is invisible to anyone who evaluates learning only during or immediately after the session.

- **The magnitude is substantial.** After one week: the group that studied once and tested three times (STTT) retained 56%. The group that studied four times (SSSS) retained 42%. The testing group spent LESS time with the material (one study session vs. four) and retained MORE. This is not a marginal effect -- it is a 33% improvement from a single procedural change.

- **Students predict the opposite.** When asked to predict their future performance, students consistently predicted that repeated study (SSSS) would produce better results than repeated testing (STTT). They were wrong. The testing effect is counterintuitive -- learners believe restudying is more effective because it produces the feeling of familiarity, which they mistake for learning.

- **The mechanism: retrieval IS encoding.** The act of attempting to retrieve information from memory -- the effortful search, the reconstruction, the production of an answer -- is itself a powerful encoding event. Each successful retrieval strengthens the memory trace. Each unsuccessful retrieval (the tip-of-the-tongue search) still activates the surrounding associative network. The test is not a measure of learning. The test IS learning.

## Methodology

Two experiments using a between-subjects design. In Experiment 1, participants studied a prose passage and then either restudied it (SS) or took a free recall test (ST). Retention was measured by free recall at 5 minutes, 2 days, or 1 week (different groups at each interval). In Experiment 2, participants studied a passage and then completed three additional learning periods: three additional study sessions (SSSS), three recall tests (STTT), one study + two tests (SSTT), or one study + one test + one study (SSTS). Retention was measured by free recall at 5 minutes or 1 week.

## Relevance to Learn Mode

**Principle:** #5 -- Push Up the Stack
**Specific connection:** This paper provides the primary evidence for the encoding stack's second level (Retrieve, 55%). The tested group's 56% retention after one week -- compared to 42% for the restudied group -- is the empirical anchor for the stack's Retrieve level. More broadly, the testing effect is the mechanism that makes the entire encoding stack work: every level above Receive requires the learner to retrieve, generate, or produce something. Each of these acts is a testing event in disguise. The specification's generation prompts ("In your own words..."), difficulty gates ("Before reading on, what do you think?"), and callback threads ("Remember when we discussed X?") are all implementations of the testing effect -- creating retrieval events within the flow of learning.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** The definitive demonstration with the crossover interaction. Other testing effect papers exist, but Roediger and Karpicke's 2006 study is the cleanest: the crossover at 5 minutes vs. 1 week shows that testing looks WORSE at short intervals and BETTER at long intervals. This is the illusion of learning made visible -- and it explains why learners and instructors systematically undervalue testing and overvalue restudying.

## Key Quotes

> "Repeated testing produced a large positive effect on long-term retention, whereas repeated studying produced no benefit beyond the immediate assessment."

> "Students' predictions of their own future performance were negatively correlated with their actual performance. They predicted that repeated study would be more effective, yet repeated testing produced superior retention."

> "Taking a test is not merely a neutral assessment of what has been learned. The act of retrieval itself modifies the memory trace, typically making it more accessible in the future."

## Limitations

The experiments used prose passages -- connected, meaningful text. The testing effect has since been demonstrated across many material types, but the specific retention percentages (56% vs. 42%) should not be treated as universal constants. The effect sizes will vary with material difficulty, delay interval, type of test (free recall vs. recognition), and individual differences. The study also does not address how testing should be integrated into ongoing learning (as opposed to being administered in discrete sessions) -- a design problem that Learn Mode's "woven retrieval" approach addresses.
