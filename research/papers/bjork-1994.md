# Memory and Metamemory Considerations in the Training of Human Beings

**Author(s):** Robert A. Bjork (UCLA)
**Published:** In J. Metcalfe & A. P. Shimamura (Eds.), *Metacognition: Knowing about Knowing* (pp. 185-205). MIT Press, 1994
**Field:** Cognitive Psychology / Metacognition / Learning Science

---

## Abstract

Bjork presents the critical distinction between learning and performance -- a distinction that has profound implications for the design of training and instruction. Current performance during training is a poor index of whether long-term learning has occurred. Conditions that maximize performance during training (massed practice, constant conditions, predictable sequencing) often minimize long-term retention and transfer. Conversely, conditions that introduce difficulties during training (spacing, variability, interleaving) often reduce performance during training while maximizing long-term learning. This dissociation between learning and performance means that learners, instructors, and training designers are systematically misled by the most intuitive metric available to them: how well the training seems to be going.

## Key Findings

- **Learning and performance are dissociable.** This is the foundational claim. Performance is what you can observe during training. Learning is a relatively permanent change in knowledge or behavior that supports long-term retention and transfer. These two things can move in opposite directions: training conditions that produce rapid performance improvement often produce rapid forgetting, while conditions that produce slow, effortful performance often produce durable learning.

- **The illusion of knowing.** Learners judge their own learning primarily by the fluency of processing during study. If the material feels familiar and easy to process, learners rate their learning as high. But fluency is a consequence of recency and repetition, not of durable encoding. The result: learners are systematically overconfident about learning from conditions that produce fluent processing (massed practice, re-reading) and systematically underconfident about learning from conditions that produce effortful processing (spaced practice, testing).

- **Implications for training design.** Training that is optimized for how it looks during training (fast acquisition, few errors, high trainee satisfaction) will often be sub-optimal for long-term retention and transfer. Training that is optimized for long-term learning (spaced practice, interleaving, desirable difficulties) will often look worse during training -- slower acquisition, more errors, lower trainee satisfaction.

- **Metacognitive failure is the default.** The chapter makes clear that poor metacognitive monitoring is not a bug in human cognition -- it is the default. People have poor insight into their own learning processes. They believe they learn best from conditions that feel effective (re-reading, massed practice) and resist conditions that feel difficult (spacing, testing). Without external correction, learners will systematically choose inferior learning strategies.

## Methodology

This is a theoretical chapter synthesizing experimental evidence from laboratory studies of: (a) spacing vs. massing effects; (b) contextual interference (varying vs. constant practice conditions); (c) judgments of learning (JOLs) compared with actual retention; (d) metacognitive monitoring accuracy across domains. The chapter integrates findings from motor learning, verbal learning, and skill acquisition.

## Relevance to Learn Mode

**Principles:** #1 -- Detect, Don't Ask; #6 -- Calibrate in Real-Time
**Specific connection:** This paper provides the core justification for two specification decisions. First, the profiler's behavioral detection (Principle 1): if people cannot accurately assess their own learning, asking them "how do you learn best?" will produce unreliable answers. The specification must observe behavior, not collect self-report. Second, real-time calibration (Principle 6): if the learner's subjective experience of difficulty is a poor guide to actual learning, the specification cannot rely on "does this feel right?" as a calibration signal. Instead, it must use comprehension pulses and behavioral signals to estimate the learner's actual state, recognizing that the learner's own estimate may be systematically wrong.
**What this chapter provides that others don't:** The theoretical framework that connects metacognitive failure to training design. Dunning and Kruger (1999) demonstrated that people misjudge their own competence. Bjork demonstrates that people misjudge their own learning in real-time, and that this misjudgment has specific, predictable consequences for the learning strategies they choose. This is the bridge between metacognitive research and instruction design.

## Key Quotes

> "Current performance is a treacherous guide to whether learning has been achieved. Conditions that enhance performance during acquisition can fail to support or even undermine long-term retention and transfer."

> "Learners are not in a good position to assess whether a given condition of practice is effective for long-term learning. Their judgments are unduly influenced by the current ease of processing, which may reflect recency and repetition rather than understanding."

> "The implications for training are sobering: the conditions that feel most effective to the learner are often the conditions that produce the least durable learning."

## Limitations

The chapter was published in 1994, before the large-scale testing effect studies of the 2000s (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006) and before the meta-analytic confirmation of the spacing effect (Cepeda et al., 2006). The theoretical framework is sound and was subsequently confirmed, but the evidence base presented in the chapter is drawn from smaller, earlier studies. The chapter also does not address how to implement desirable difficulties in conversational or AI-mediated learning contexts -- these did not exist at the time of writing.
