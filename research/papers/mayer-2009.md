# Multimedia Learning

**Author(s):** Richard E. Mayer (University of California, Santa Barbara)
**Published:** 2nd edition, Cambridge University Press, 2009
**ISBN:** 978-0521735353
**Field:** Educational Psychology / Cognitive Science

---

## Abstract

Mayer presents the Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (CTML), a framework for understanding how people learn from words and pictures. The theory rests on three assumptions derived from cognitive science: (1) humans process visual and auditory information through separate channels (dual channel assumption, building on Paivio and Baddeley); (2) each channel has limited processing capacity (limited capacity assumption); (3) meaningful learning requires active cognitive processing -- selecting relevant information, organizing it into coherent mental representations, and integrating it with existing knowledge (active processing assumption). From these assumptions and hundreds of controlled experiments, Mayer derives 12 principles of multimedia learning that specify how to design instructional materials for optimal learning.

## Key Findings

- **The multimedia principle.** People learn better from words and pictures than from words alone. This is the foundational finding -- it holds across dozens of experiments and replicates Paivio's dual coding theory at the instructional level.

- **The modality principle.** People learn better from animation + narration than from animation + on-screen text. The visual channel can be overloaded by simultaneous animation and text; distributing information across channels (visual for pictures, auditory for words) reduces this overload.

- **The redundancy principle.** People learn better from animation + narration than from animation + narration + on-screen text. Adding redundant text to an already-narrated animation overloads the visual channel without adding new information. Less can be more when channel capacity is the bottleneck.

- **The coherence principle.** People learn better when extraneous material is excluded -- interesting but irrelevant details, background music, decorative illustrations. Extraneous material consumes limited processing capacity without contributing to learning.

- **The signaling principle.** People learn better when cues are added that highlight the organization of essential material. Signaling directs attention to the most important information, reducing the processing demands of selection.

- **The spatial contiguity principle.** People learn better when corresponding words and pictures are presented near each other on the page rather than far apart.

- **The temporal contiguity principle.** People learn better when corresponding words and pictures are presented simultaneously rather than successively.

- **The segmenting principle.** People learn better when a complex lesson is presented in learner-paced segments rather than as a continuous unit.

- **The pre-training principle.** People learn better from a complex lesson when they know the names and characteristics of key components before the lesson begins.

## Methodology

The book synthesizes approximately 20 years of controlled experiments from Mayer's laboratory and others. The typical experiment: participants study a multimedia lesson (e.g., how lightning forms, how a bicycle pump works) under different presentation conditions (words only vs. words + pictures, animation + narration vs. animation + text, etc.). Learning is measured by a transfer test requiring participants to use the learned information to solve novel problems. The dependent variable is meaningful learning (transfer), not just retention (recall).

## Relevance to Learn Mode

**Principle:** #4 -- Dual Channel Minimum
**Specific connection:** Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning is the operational bridge between Paivio's dual coding theory (the theoretical foundation) and Learn Mode's channel adaptation system (the implementation). Paivio explains WHY dual coding works (two independent memory systems). Mayer explains HOW to design for it (12 principles governing the relationship between channels). The specification's requirement that all key information be encoded in at least two channels draws on both: Paivio for the theoretical justification and Mayer for the practical design constraints. The coherence principle ("every element pays rent") is adopted directly as a specification rule.
**What this book provides that others don't:** The practical design framework. Paivio's dual coding theory is abstract -- it describes cognitive architecture but does not specify how to design instruction. Mayer translates dual coding into testable, actionable principles: where to place pictures relative to words, when to use narration vs. text, how to avoid overloading either channel. These principles directly inform how Learn Mode's channel adaptation system structures its output.

## Key Quotes

> "People learn better from words and pictures than from words alone. This multimedia principle is one of the most well-supported findings in the science of instruction."

> "The purpose of multimedia instruction is not to deliver information but to promote active cognitive processing: selecting, organizing, and integrating."

> "Extraneous material competes for limited cognitive resources. When those resources are consumed by interesting but irrelevant details, fewer resources are available for the essential processing that produces learning."

## Limitations

Mayer's experiments typically measure learning at immediate or short-delay intervals (minutes to hours). The long-term retention benefits of multimedia encoding are theoretically supported by Paivio's dual coding but not extensively tested in Mayer's program. The principles were developed primarily with science explanations (lightning, bicycle pumps, car brakes) -- their application to other content types (narrative, procedural, tacit knowledge) requires design inference. The text-based AI context is also substantially different from Mayer's experimental paradigm (which often used animation and narration); adapting the principles to text-only delivery with descriptive imagery requires creative application rather than direct transfer.
